How to read this map

What you're looking at. Each tile is a formal classification — a formal partition of physical or mathematical content along discrete axes. Rows are sectors of physics; tiles within a row are the classifications in that sector.

Tile anatomy

The big 2–3 letter mark is the classification's symbol (SM, GUT, 10W for ten-fold way, MGr for modified gravity, …). The left-edge stripe is the category colour: blue = structural, green = phenomenon, amber = hybrid. Top-right shows cell count; if there are falsified predictions inside, you'll see a red ⚠. The thin bar at the bottom is the predictive yield: green = confirmed, amber = tension, gray = not yet tested, red = falsified, purple = retro-explanatory.

The little grid of squares inside a tile is its cells, one square each. A filled square is a position physically realized; a faint hatched square is a position the classification's own structure forbids (the dual of an empty cell — nature rules it out); a bold accent cross-hatch is a position the structure predicts should exist but that nothing fills yet — the empty-cell prediction. A plain pale square is a position not yet authored. A small ⇄ by the count means the same physical content recurs in another classification — visible without clicking. A tile carrying empty-cell predictions shows a count chip (e.g. 2 gaps); today every such count reads zero, which is correct — those positions populate when a cross-section is built, not before. The small ? chip near the yield bar flips the tile over: the back lists the open frontiers recorded as bearing on that classification (a red ?N means there are N), each clickable through to its full frontier record; tiles with none say so explicitly — under this map's methodology that absence is itself recorded information. A classification's sidebar record has the same flip button. And once you're zoomed in (past ~135%), the marks on a tile explain themselves: click the symbol, the counts, the ⚠ flag, the cell grid, the chips, the yield bar, or the footer for a popup describing exactly that mark.

Drill-down

Hover a tile for a tooltip preview. Click a tile to open its details in the right sidebar — description, classification axes, cells, predictions (with a status filter), cross-classification edges, citations. Clicking also lights the tile and dims the rest; click further tiles to light several at once, and click a lit tile to switch it off — so exactly the part of the map you want to focus on stays lit. Esc, the sidebar's ×, ⌂ Home, the Map of Physics title (top-left), Browse → Home, or a plain click on the map background restores the full map. With the cross-section builder open, clicking tiles adds them to (or removes them from) the cross-section, and the selected classifications light up the same way. The map stays visible the whole time. Inside the sidebar, you can click any cell to see its full record (axis values, examples, predictions tied to that cell, citations). A breadcrumb at the top of the sidebar lets you walk back up.

Physical phenomena

Open Browse ▾ and choose Phenomena (or use the sidebar quick-bar). Each toggle highlights the classifications that contain that thing, in its category's colour. A tile glows with one ring per matching category — so a tile hit by both Particles and Cosmological will show both rings. Phenomena that map to the same exact set of classifications have been combined; the merging itself is information. all on / all off reset. The phen↔phen and cross-class overlay toggles — in the View toolbox and in the legend's layer toggles — draw arrows on the map (phen↔phen shows the 7 phenomenon-to-phenomenon relationships, with compact-astrophysical-objects as the visual hub).

Finding a specific thing

The search box at the top of the toolbar searches all 484 cells. Type black holes, gluons, AdS/CFT, topological insulator, or anything else. Results are grouped by classification with cell snippets; clicking a result unfolds its full record right there in the list (scroll inside it for long content), and the ↗ on any row — or on a group header — opens the full page for that cell or classification. For the terms used to describe how the map itself is organised (open frontier, discharge status, candidate-foundational, …) open the Glossary via the sidebar quick-bar or Browse menu.

Layers + filters

The view bar above the map reorganises it. Its three group by chips stack: one on = flat rows (sector / category / closure), two on = a cross-grid (1st = rows, 2nd = columns — e.g. sector × category), all three on = the 3-D view (sector rows · category columns · closure inside each cell). The Tools button beside it holds the map tools — Spotlight, an all-tiles on/off switch, copy-link (to save a view), reset — and the overlay toggles. In any cross-grid an empty intersection renders as a hatched gap cell — a recorded fact that no classification exists at that intersection in the data, a Mendeleev-flavored statement about where formalization is thin. Spotlight dims tiles whose predictions don't match a status. The overlay toggles live in the same toolbox (and in the legend).

Shared axes

Inside any classification's sidebar record, the axis names are clickable. Click one and every classification on the map carrying an axis of the same name lights up; the ×N badge next to the name tells you how many before you click. Click the axis again (from any carrier's record) to switch the lighting off. This recovers the old map's column intuition as an interaction instead of a position — the canonical demo is cartan-type on any of the five ADE classifications, which lights the whole quintet. The legend's quick-spotlight list has a one-click version.

Map controls

Drag anywhere on the map background to pan. Scroll / pinch over the map to zoom (cursor-anchored). The control row at the top: + / − step zoom, ⛶ fits the whole map to the page, ↶ undoes map changes one step at a time, ⟲ resets the map to its default (sector rows, every layer off, fit to page), the View button opens the grouping toolbox, and the Tools button beside it holds the map tools and overlays. The map always loads in its default state; to save a view, use copy link to this view in the toolbox — the link restores it exactly. The open-questions landing (frontier cards) is under Browse → Open questions; its inline link, the title, or Browse → Home bring you back. Keyboard: + / − step zoom, 0 or f fit to page, Esc to clear a selection. The sidebar collapses with the › tab on its left edge.

Cross-sections and empty cells

The deepest use of the map is to lay several classifications side by side along a shared axis and read off what the combined structure implies. Where the same physical content sits at corresponding positions in two classifications, that recurrence is itself information (the ⇄ marker flags tiles where it happens; ⇄ Compare and a classification's cross-classification edges in its sidebar let you trace it). Where the combined structure implies a position should be occupied and nothing occupies it, that gap is an empty-cell prediction; where the structure forbids a position, that is a structural exclusion. The map keeps the organization anchored to the literature, so the pattern-finding and the tests of candidate unification frameworks are yours to run — the map supplies the organized ground, not the conclusions. The cross-section builder constructs these multi-classification views directly: open ⧉ Build cross-section in the toolbar, lay two or more classifications along a shared axis, and read off the recurrences and gaps the combined structure implies. Recurrences, exclusions, and (when present) predicted gaps remain visible per tile as well.

The experimental layer

Beyond the formal classifications, the map records the experimental programs targeting each frontier and the characteristic numerical commitments behind them. Click ⚙ Programs for the catalogue of 19 experimental programs (collaborations, accelerators, surveys, observatories). Click ⇄ Compare programs to see where two programs share targets — competing-prediction adjudication, orthogonal-channel measurement, or complementary reach. Click ▲▼ Scales for the cross-map catalogue of every quantitative bound and characteristic scale recorded in the map, grouped by physical dimension. Inside any frontier's sidebar card you'll see its conditional consequences ("if real, implies …") — what gets forced into the map if the frontier resolves a particular way. The ✦ Connect to AI button at the right end of the toolbar opens a guided setup for using the catalogue as a tool inside an AI chat, through its read-only query surface.

What's in the data

83 nodes · 232 edges · 30 formal classifications · 484 cells · 209 predictions (230 inclusive of cell-scoped) · 103 cross-classification edges · 17 falsifications · 8 phenomenon-to-phenomenon edges · 19 experimental programs · 14 open frontiers carrying conditional consequences (what becomes forced if the frontier resolves one way or another) · 40 experimental-coverage relations targeting cells and frontiers · 288 characteristic scales recorded across mass, energy, length, time, coupling, dimensionless quantity, ratio, and deviation in σ · 6 program-pair comparisons documenting shared coverage. The dataset is read live from the project’s GitHub repository when the page loads.
