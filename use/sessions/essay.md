# What this catalogue is, and why a working physicist might care

*A use-side essay for the Map of Physics. Roughly 2,200 words. It threads three worked examples — drawn from the sessions in [`sessions/`](sessions/) and verified against the live catalogue — into an answer to the question the project's other documents mostly leave implicit: what kind of thing is this, and what can it do that you can't already do in your head or with a review article? Written in the physicist-facing vocabulary discipline.*

## The shape of the thing

Mendeleev's periodic table did something a list of the known elements did not: it had structure, and the structure made claims. Gaps in the rows were not absences of knowledge but predictions — eka-silicon had to exist, and had to have roughly these properties, because the pattern said so. The table was a piece of organized physics that could be *read for consequences*.

The Map of Physics is an attempt at the same move for modern physics, and the comparison is worth taking literally rather than as decoration. The catalogue organizes physical and mathematical content into formal classifications — partitions along discrete axes, where each cell is a specific theory, particle, phase, or observation. On top of that backbone it records the open questions of the field as *frontiers*, each carrying the candidate resolutions that might close it, the experimental programs addressing it, and — this is the part that earns the Mendeleev comparison — the structural consequences that would be *forced* if a given resolution turned out to be right.

It is important to be precise about where the prediction lives. The catalogue does not divine the contents of empty cells the way Mendeleev read off atomic weights. The predictive move sits one level up, at the frontier: an open question, taken together with a specific candidate resolution, forces particular cells elsewhere in the catalogue to take particular values. The "empty cell" being predicted is a not-yet-settled property, and the frontier's resolution is what would fill it, in a forced direction, with a citable derivation. That is a more honest claim than "the table predicts the next element," and it is the claim the catalogue actually supports.

The reason any of this matters to a working physicist is not that the catalogue knows physics you don't. It is that it holds the physics as *structure you can query*, with every entry traced to a named source — and a structure you can query answers a different class of question than free recall does. A general-purpose model, or your own memory, can recount the leptogenesis story or the strong-CP problem fluently. What neither can do reliably is tell you, from a fixed and citation-traced record, exactly which other entries a confirmation would force, exactly which experiments are recorded as addressing a frontier and whether each measures or merely bounds, and exactly what the catalogue declines to claim. The three examples below are each an instance of that difference.

## First move — reading an open problem for what it forces

Take the matter–antimatter asymmetry. Every graduate student knows the outline: the observed baryon-to-photon ratio is η ≈ 6.1 × 10⁻¹⁰ (Planck 2020; PDG 2024), the Standard Model satisfies Sakharov's three conditions in principle, and yet it falls quantitatively short of the observed asymmetry by orders of magnitude. The catalogue records this not as a narrative but as an open question with a specific kind of failure attached: a mechanism that runs but does not reach, rather than one that is missing. It lists four candidate resolutions and, usefully, how far each has been carried — leptogenesis substantially developed and constrained by neutrino-mass data, extended electroweak baryogenesis constrained by the LHC, Affleck–Dine early-stage, GUT-scale baryogenesis constrained by inflationary dilution.

Now ask the question a review leaves implicit: *if leptogenesis is the right answer, what does that force?* The catalogue answers structurally. Conditional on the asymmetry being generated by CP-asymmetric decays of heavy Majorana right-handed neutrinos, converted to baryon number through electroweak sphalerons (Fukugita–Yanagida 1986; Davidson–Nardi–Nir 2008), two specific entries elsewhere are pinned down. The Dirac-vs-Majorana question in the neutrino-sector classification — presently carried as an open, predicted-only property — is forced to resolve to Majorana, because leptogenesis requires lepton-number-violating Majorana masses by construction. And the leptogenesis entry itself is promoted from predicted-only to confirmed-by-indirect-evidence: the mechanism is not directly testable in a laboratory, but a confirmed asymmetry of this origin settles its status. The conditional carries a number, too — the lightest right-handed Majorana neutrino must satisfy M₁ ≳ 10⁹ GeV, the Davidson–Ibarra bound (2002), the lower limit for thermal leptogenesis to reproduce the observed η.

That is the Mendeleev move performed on real entries: open question → an empirical bound it must explain → a candidate resolution → specific cells the resolution would force, in a stated direction → a required scale with the paper that derived it. The "empty cell" is the unsettled Dirac-vs-Majorana property; the frontier's resolution is what would fill it. A free-recall model can tell you leptogenesis prefers Majorana neutrinos. It cannot hand you the forced cell, the direction, the bound, and the citation as a single traced object, because that object is structure the catalogue holds and prose does not.

## Second move — reading a frontier's experimental coverage

The strong-CP problem is a cleaner case for a different question: not *what would be forced* but *how is this actually being pressed in the lab, and do the experiments measure or merely bound?* The frontier's anchor is the bound $\bar\theta_{\mathrm{QCD}} < 10^{-10}$ (Abel et al. 2020), and the reason it stays open is recorded as an organizing-structure failure — gauge symmetry alone simply does not constrain the angle to be small.

Ask for the experimental coverage and the catalogue returns two independent programs, both running, with no shared apparatus and no shared observable. Axion haloscopes — ADMX, HAYSTAC, MADMAX, and others — pursue the direct-detection channel, converting a galactic-halo axion to a microwave photon in a resonant cavity (Sikivie 1983) and bounding the axion–photon coupling at $g_{a\gamma\gamma} \lesssim 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{GeV^{-1}}$, with ADMX Run 1B already at DFSZ sensitivity over the few-μeV band (Bartram et al. 2021). Next-generation neutron-EDM searches pursue the indirect channel: the Crewther–DiVecchia–Veneziano–Witten relation turns an upper bound on the neutron EDM straight into an upper bound on $\bar\theta_{\mathrm{QCD}}$, so the PSI measurement that gives the frontier its recorded bound is heading, via the n2EDM successor, toward $|d_n| \lesssim 10^{-27}\ e\!\cdot\!\mathrm{cm}$ (Ayres et al. 2021). One frontier, two channels, attacking the same Peccei–Quinn resolution from opposite ends — one bounding the postulated axion's coupling, the other bounding the order parameter the mechanism is meant to relax.

The instructive part is what happens when you push further and ask whether the two channels *discriminate* between competing predictions. The answer comes back empty, and the emptiness is the content. Both channels are recorded as bounds-setting, not measurement-discriminating, because the candidate axion models predict *relations* among the breaking scale, the axion mass, and the coupling — a hypothesis class, not rival point values you could set side by side and have an experiment choose between. The catalogue records no competing point predictions here on purpose; inventing them would assert structure the literature does not have. A model eager to be helpful will often manufacture a crisp "experiment A predicts X, experiment B predicts Y" story on request. The catalogue's refusal to do so — its willingness to return an informative nothing — is exactly the discipline that makes the rest of it trustworthy. (For completeness, the catalogue also records what a *detection* would force: a new classification of the QCD-axion model space, with the Peccei–Quinn breaking scale in the open window $f_a \sim 10^{8}$–$10^{12}\ \mathrm{GeV}$.)

## Third move — reading the negative space

The first two examples are about what the catalogue affirms and what it conditionally forces. The third is about what it has crossed out, and it turns out the crossings-out have a shape of their own.

The catalogue records seventeen falsified predictions, and they fall into two recorded kinds. Some are specific beyond-Standard-Model hypotheses that were ruled out: minimal non-supersymmetric SU(5)'s proton-decay rate, killed by Super-Kamiokande's lower bound of τ > 2.4 × 10³⁴ years on p → e⁺π⁰ (Takenaka et al. 2020) — orders of magnitude beyond the original 10²⁹–10³¹-year prediction (Georgi–Glashow 1974); MACHOs as the bulk of the halo, bounded below 8% by EROS-2 microlensing (Tisserand et al. 2007); pure MOND at cluster scales, undone by the Bullet Cluster's offset between lensing mass and baryonic mass (Clowe et al. 2006); the self-accelerating DGP branch; generic TeVeS and Horndeski classes, whose predicted deviation of the gravitational-wave speed from $c$ was closed off by GW170817 to better than a part in 10¹⁵; the natural-parameter Brans–Dicke prediction, pinned by the Cassini measurement of the PPN parameter γ (Bertotti et al. 2003); the deeply-bound H-dibaryon (Jaffe 1977), found unbound by lattice QCD; and primordial black holes as all of the dark matter in the LIGO mass window, excluded to a fraction below 10⁻³ (Hütsi et al. 2021).

The others are over-claimed empirical signals that more data excluded: the CDMS-II silicon three-event WIMP hint, closed by SuperCDMS; the EDGES 21-cm absorption feature, not reproduced by SARAS 3; the DAMA/LIBRA annual modulation's WIMP interpretation, excluded by XENONnT, LZ, and the NaI-target ANAIS-112; the Heidelberg-Moscow neutrinoless-double-beta-decay claim, buried by GERDA's limit more than an order of magnitude above it; the LSND sterile-neutrino interpretation, closed by IceCube and MicroBooNE; and the celebrated quantized-Majorana-conductance result, retracted in 2021 after the apparent 2e²/h quantization proved disorder-induced.

Two things are worth seeing here. First, the regularity: *every* entry in the falsified bucket is either an overreaching BSM hypothesis or an over-claimed specific signal. No settled-physics prediction appears, because settled physics, by construction, does not get falsified — the bucket is a record of where the field's ambition outran its evidence, and that is a structural fact about which classes of claim enter it. Second, the catalogue's care at the boundary. It distinguishes the falsified *specific claim* from the still-open *general question* in every case where they differ: the DAMA modulation itself is a real 12.9σ effect even though its WIMP reading is dead; the Majorana neutrino hypothesis remains entirely open even though the Heidelberg-Moscow positive detection is gone; Majorana-zero-mode platforms continue even though the 2018 quantization claim was retracted. A catalogue that recorded "0νββ: falsified" would be worse than useless. One that records "this specific positive claim: falsified; the underlying hypothesis: open" is doing the work.

## What the three have in common

The spine shared by all three moves is that the catalogue answers *structural* questions — what is forced, what is bounded, what is measured versus merely excluded, what has been ruled out and what that says about the field — and it answers them from a fixed record with every claim traced to a named source. That is a different service than a fluent retelling. The leptogenesis example hands you a forced cell and a derived scale as one object. The strong-CP example hands you two channels, their reach, their posture, and an honest empty result where a contest would be invented. The falsified bucket hands you a shape in the negative space and the discipline that keeps the shape meaningful.

None of this replaces knowing the physics. It is scaffolding for asking sharper questions of it — and a guard-rail against the confident fabrication that an unconstrained model will offer when the honest answer is "the literature does not say." If that is useful to you, the worked sessions in [`sessions/`](sessions/) show each move end to end, the recipes in [`recipes.md`](recipes.md) are the patterns to try on your own questions, and the guided entry point lets you ask the catalogue directly without setting anything up.
