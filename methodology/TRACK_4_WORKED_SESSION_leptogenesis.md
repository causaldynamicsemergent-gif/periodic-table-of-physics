# Worked session — "What does this catalogue say about baryogenesis?"

*A curated use-side artifact for the Map of Physics. Demonstrates the frontier-level prediction move — the catalogue's analog of reading an empty cell off the pattern — performed end-to-end on the live substrate, in physicist prose. Pairs with the query recipe at the foot of this file. Drafted from a real session against data v99; lightly edited for readability per `TRACK_4_USE_SIDE_ARTIFACTS.md` §1.4.*

---

## The recipe this session illustrates

> **If you want to know:** what an open problem in physics would *force* to be true if a particular resolution of it held — and what is already being done to test it —
> **the move is:** open the question as a frontier, read its empirical bound and the structural reason it stays open, then follow its forced consequences into the specific classifications and cells they pin down, and its experimental coverage into the programs that address it.
> **A worked example follows.** The answer lives on the baryon-asymmetry frontier card and the neutrino-sector classification it points into.

---

## The session

**Physicist:** I know the baryon-asymmetry story from textbooks — Sakharov conditions, the Standard Model falls short. Is there anything in this catalogue a review article wouldn't already give me?

**Assistant:** The catalogue treats matter–antimatter asymmetry as an *open question with structure attached*, which is a slightly different object than a review's narrative. Three things it pins down explicitly.

The empirical target it has to explain is the baryon-to-photon ratio, η ≈ 6.1 × 10⁻¹⁰ (Planck 2020; PDG 2024). The reason the question stays open is recorded as a specific kind of failure: the Standard Model satisfies all three Sakharov conditions *in principle* but extrapolates to an asymmetry many orders of magnitude too small — a mechanism that runs but doesn't reach, rather than a mechanism that's missing. And it lists four candidate resolutions with where each currently stands: leptogenesis (substantially developed, constrained by neutrino-mass data), extended electroweak baryogenesis (developed, constrained by LHC), Affleck–Dine (early-stage, partly developed), and GUT-scale baryogenesis (constrained by inflationary dilution). That last column — *how far along each candidate is* — is the part a review usually leaves implicit.

**Physicist:** Take leptogenesis. If it's the right answer, what does that actually force?

**Assistant:** This is where the catalogue does something a narrative doesn't. It records the resolution as a conditional with explicit consequences: *if* η is generated by CP-asymmetric decays of heavy Majorana right-handed neutrinos, converted to baryon number through electroweak sphalerons (Fukugita–Yanagida 1986; Davidson–Nardi–Nir 2008), *then* two specific entries elsewhere in the catalogue are forced.

First, the Dirac-vs-Majorana question in the neutrino-sector classification — currently carried as predicted-only — is forced to resolve to Majorana. Leptogenesis requires lepton-number-violating Majorana masses by construction, so a confirmed leptogenetic origin would settle that cell, not leave it open. Second, the leptogenesis entry itself in that classification would move from predicted-only to confirmed-by-indirect-evidence — the mechanism isn't directly testable in a laboratory, but a confirmed asymmetry of this origin resolves its status.

And the conditional carries a number: the lightest right-handed Majorana neutrino must satisfy M₁ ≳ 10⁹ GeV — the Davidson–Ibarra bound (2002), the lower limit for thermal leptogenesis to reproduce the observed η. So the chain is: an open question → a measured bound it must explain → a candidate resolution → two specific cells that the resolution would force to specific values → a mass scale the resolution requires, with the paper that derived it. That is the catalogue reading a consequence off the structure, the way the periodic table reads a missing element off its row — here the "empty cell" is a not-yet-settled property (Dirac vs Majorana) that one resolution would fill in a forced direction.

**Physicist:** So which experiment settles it?

**Assistant:** Here the honest answer is the interesting one. The catalogue records exactly one program addressing this frontier: next-generation electric-dipole-moment searches, via the electron-EDM channel (JILA HfF⁺, |d_e| < 4.1 × 10⁻³⁰ e·cm, Roussy et al. 2023; ACME continuing toward ~10⁻³⁰). The BSM CP-violating phases needed to source the asymmetry generically feed the electron EDM at loop level, so tightening that bound closes off candidate scenarios (Cesarotti et al. 2019).

But the catalogue marks this coverage as *bounds-setting, not measurement-discriminating* — and it's worth being clear why, because it's a curatorial choice rather than an omission. The four candidate mechanisms predict *ranges* for the CP-violating phases, not single competing values you could place side by side and have the experiment choose between. Leptogenesis's CP asymmetry depends on the whole heavy-neutrino mass spectrum and Yukawa structure with large freedom; extended electroweak baryogenesis spans roughly 10⁻²⁸ down past 10⁻³¹ e·cm across model classes. So the EDM bound *excludes regions* — it has already essentially ruled out light-stop electroweak baryogenesis — rather than adjudicating between two point predictions. The catalogue records no competing point predictions here, on purpose: inventing them would be asserting structure the literature doesn't have. If you ask it directly for an experiment that discriminates competing predictions on this frontier, it returns nothing, and that emptiness is the correct, informative answer.

**Physicist:** That's more disciplined than I expected.

**Assistant:** That discipline is the point of the thing. It will tell you what is forced, what is bounded, and where the literature stops — and it won't paper over the third one. The baryon-asymmetry frontier is a clean case: a sharp bound to explain, a resolution that forces named consequences with a derived mass scale, and one running experiment that narrows the field without yet deciding it.

## What this demonstrates

- **The prediction move lives at the frontier, performed on real entries.** Open question → empirical bound → candidate resolution → *forced consequences in specific cells* (Dirac-vs-Majorana → Majorana; leptogenesis cell → confirmed-by-indirect) → a required scale (M₁ ≳ 10⁹ GeV) with its citation. A general-purpose assistant can recount the leptogenesis story; it cannot tell you *which catalogued cells a confirmation would force, in which direction*, because that is structure the catalogue holds and free text does not.
- **Informative emptiness is a first-class result.** The frontier is addressed by one program, and that program is bounds-setting; asking for a discriminating experiment returns nothing. The session shows this as curatorial honesty — parameter-class predictions are not dressed up as competing point values.
- **The vocabulary stays physics-first.** Nothing in the physicist-facing turns names an internal field; the moves read as "open question," "empirical bound," "what it forces," "experimental coverage," "bounds-setting vs measurement-discriminating."

## The recipe, distilled (for the "How to use the map" page)

**Reading an open problem for what it forces.** Open the problem as a frontier and read three things off it: the empirical bound it must explain, the structural reason it stays open, and its candidate resolutions with how far each has come. Then follow a resolution's *forced consequences* — the catalogue records, for each candidate, which classifications and cells a confirmation would pin down and in which direction, often with a required scale and the paper that derived it. Finally read its *experimental coverage*: which running or planned programs address it, and crucially whether each is *measurement-discriminating* (adjudicating between competing predicted values) or *bounds-setting* (excluding regions of parameter space). When you ask for an experiment that discriminates competing predictions and the catalogue returns nothing, that is a real answer: the candidates supply ranges, not rival point values, and the map declines to invent them. *Worked example: the baryon-asymmetry frontier → leptogenesis → Majorana neutrinos forced, M₁ ≳ 10⁹ GeV required, electron-EDM searches setting bounds.*
