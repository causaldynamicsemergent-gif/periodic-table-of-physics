# Query recipes

*Six query patterns for working physicists who have read the [`Use the map`](./USE_THE_MAP_ESSAY.md) essay and want to try questions of their own. Draft 2026-05-27.*

---

The essay shows what the dataset answers. This file shows the shapes of the queries that get those answers. Each recipe below is a paragraph: the natural-language question a physicist might ask, the move the dataset offers in response, one worked example with citations and the quantitative values you should be able to reproduce, and the deep link into the explorer where the worked example lives. The last sentence of each recipe names the underlying lookup, so a reader who has connected the dataset's catalogue server to a chat can run the query themselves; everyone else can read the recipes as a guide to what kinds of questions the dataset is built to answer.

The recipes are not API documentation. They are patterns. Substitute your own frontier, your own observable, your own physical entity; the shape of the answer the dataset returns is what each pattern describes. Six patterns cover the cross-cutting moves the essay's worked examples demonstrated, plus two further moves the essay did not have room for.

---

## 1. Which experimental programs target this open question, and through which physical channels?

The simplest cross-cutting query and the most natural physicist starting point. Pick an open frontier of physics, a specific observable in one of the dataset's classifications, or a candidate-foundational program a community is pursuing — and surface the set of experimental programs the dataset records as addressing it. The dataset returns one entry per program, each carrying the program's sensitivity floor (the design reach for the target, written in the program's native units), the timeline (running / planned / proposed), the physical channel the program uses, and where the program's design report or proposal anchors each of these claims.

A worked instance: take the cosmological-constant frontier — whether the dark-energy equation-of-state w(z) deviates from the ΛCDM value w ≡ −1. Two programs come back. DESI is designed to reach ≥5σ resolution of a (w_0, w_a) deviation from ΛCDM at full exposure via sub-percent baryon-acoustic-oscillation standard-ruler distances across 0.1 < z < 3.5 combined with CMB priors and Type-Ia-SN luminosity distances [Adame et al. (DESI Collaboration) 2024, JCAP 02:021]. Rubin Observatory's 10-year LSST reaches σ(w_0) ≈ 0.01 and σ(w_a) ≈ 0.04 via the joint 3×2pt analysis (cosmic shear + galaxy clustering + galaxy-galaxy lensing) plus photometric supernovae plus cluster counts [The LSST Dark Energy Science Collaboration 2018, arXiv:1809.01669]. Two programs, two structurally different physical channels — a sound-horizon standard ruler on one side, a weak-lensing 3×2pt growth-vs-expansion probe on the other — both attacking the same w(z) deviation, with sensitivity floors encoded in different native units (DESI: a sigma-deviation discovery floor; Rubin: a two-sided precision on a dimensionless equation-of-state parameter).

> *Where to look:* the [cosmological-constant frontier discourse card](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/discourse/cc-frontier) — the two resolvers appear under "Targeted by," each with sensitivity, timeline, channel description, and competing-prediction list where populated.

The underlying lookup is the experimental-coverage query (tool name: `find_resolvers`); the argument is the id of the frontier, cell, or candidate-foundational program you want covered. An empty result is itself a structural fact about the dataset — see recipe 4.

## 2. Where does the same physical entity classify in more than one framework, and how do the two views relate?

A physicist who has organized a corner of physics one way can ask whether the dataset records the same entity organized a different way somewhere else, and whether the two organizations are structurally related. The dataset surfaces cross-classification relations: a directed edge from one classification to another, carrying a relation type (the target's structure *derives from* the source's; or the target *specializes* the source; or the two are *interpretation-distinct realizations* of the same content), a list of the specific cells on each side that participate, and a shared citation set establishing the relation in the literature.

A worked instance: the 7.1 keV sterile neutrino lives in two of the dataset's classifications at once. In the dark-matter candidates classification it appears as a warm-dark-matter cell whose particle-mass anchor is m_s ≈ 7.1 keV, derived from the two-body decay kinematics E_γ = m_s/2 applied to the 3.55 keV X-ray emission line first detected in stacked galaxy-cluster spectra [Bulbul et al. 2014, Astrophys. J. 789:13; Boyarsky et al. 2014, Phys. Rev. Lett. 113:251301]. In neutrino-sector phenomenology it appears as a sterile-neutrino-mixing cell whose observational-signature anchor is the 3.55 keV line itself — same observation, two attachment points, related by the two-body-decay relation. The cross-classification edge that formalizes this is a *derives-from* relation whose source-side pinned cells are on the dark-matter side: under the sterile-neutrino-as-warm-DM hypothesis, the dark-matter-candidate structure reads off the neutrino-sector flavor framework rather than being independently postulated. The same pattern repeats one mass scale up, at the eV scale, with the LSND-falsified and BEST-gallium-remaining-tension parameter regions (the essay's example 4 worked this case in detail).

> *Where to look:* the [cross-classification edge between dark-matter candidates and neutrino-sector phenomenology](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/edge/edge-xc-dm-derives-nu-sector-partial) — pinned source-side cells, target-side cells, relation type, status, and the shared citation set.

The underlying lookup is the cross-classification edge query (tool name: `find_cross_classification`), with `from` and `to` parameters when you have the two classifications in mind, or `targeted_by` when you want every edge incident on a specific cell.

## 3. If this open question resolves a particular way, what gets forced?

A conditional structural query a working physicist might naturally ask but a literature search will not answer cleanly. An open question often has several candidate resolutions in the literature, each carrying its own structural commitments — a new particle a new framework requires, a new axis on an existing classification, a new relation between existing classifications, a quantitative scale the candidate's mechanism fixes. The dataset records each candidate resolution as an *if-real-implies* entry on the open frontier or candidate-foundational program node, with the resolution's condition prose anchored to its literature, an enumerated list of the structural consequences the resolution would force, and a quantitative scale on each consequence where the literature supplies one.

A worked instance: the dark-matter frontier carries three documented competing resolutions, and the dataset records what each would force structurally. *If* the QCD axion produced by the Peccei–Quinn mechanism constitutes the cold dark matter, the dark-matter-candidates classification's axion cells derive from an extension of the Standard-Model gauge content by the spontaneously-broken U(1)_PQ symmetry, and the Peccei–Quinn-breaking scale f_a falls in the misalignment-mechanism window 10^11–10^12 GeV that produces the observed relic density Ω_DM h² ≈ 0.12 at typical initial misalignment angle [Preskill–Wise–Wilczek 1983, Phys. Lett. B 120:127; Abbott–Sikivie 1983, Phys. Lett. B 120:133]. *If* primordial black holes constitute a substantial dark-matter fraction in the currently-allowed asteroid-mass window 10^−17 to 10^−12 M_⊙, the dark-matter-candidates classification derives from the compact-astrophysical-objects classification rather than being independently postulated, with the cross-classification carrying an axis mapping between production mechanism and object class [Carr–Kühnel–Sandstad 2016, Phys. Rev. D 94:083504; Carr–Kühnel 2020, Annu. Rev. Nucl. Part. Sci. 70:355]. *If* the apparent dark-matter signal at galactic scales is in fact a modification of gravity at low accelerations, the entire dark-matter-candidates classification is specialized by — under a strong reading, structurally excluded by — the modified-gravity-alternatives classification, since the particle-dark-matter cells lose load-bearingness under the MOND interpretation [Milgrom 1983, Astrophys. J. 270:365; McGaugh–Lelli–Schombert 2016, Phys. Rev. Lett. 117:201101; Skordis–Złośnik 2021, Phys. Rev. Lett. 127:161302]. Three candidate resolutions, three different structural commitments, each documented separately — a working physicist can read off what an empirical settlement would mean for the rest of the catalogue.

> *Where to look:* the [dark-matter frontier discourse card](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/discourse/dark-matter) — the "If real, implies…" section, with one expandable group per resolution and one row per forced consequence, plus the quantitative scale on each consequence where the literature supplies one.

The underlying lookup is the conditional-consequences query (tool name: `find_signal_implications`), called with the carrier id when you want one frontier's full set, or with no argument for a dataset-wide scan of the fourteen carriers currently encoded.

## 4. Which experiments adjudicate between competing theoretical predictions on the same target, and what are the predictions?

A sharper version of recipe 1, restricted to cases where the dataset has encoded competing point predictions side by side. This is the cleanest cross-cutting query the dataset offers: where two or more theoretical programs predict different values for the same observable, and an experimental program addresses the gap between them. Each entry returns the experimental program's sensitivity, the timeline, and the per-program list — each candidate program with its predicted central value, its uncertainty, and the citation that anchors the program-internal calculation that produced the prediction.

A worked instance: the muon anomalous magnetic moment a_μ has two competing Standard-Model-side calculations encoded as candidate programs, side by side. Theory Initiative 2020 (dispersive method, anchored by e⁺e⁻ → hadrons cross-section data) gives a_μ^SM = (116 591 810 ± 43) × 10⁻¹¹ [Aoyama et al. 2020, Phys. Rep. 887:1]. BMW 2021 (lattice-QCD ab initio calculation of the leading-order hadronic vacuum polarization) gives a_μ^SM ≈ (116 591 954 ± 55) × 10⁻¹¹ [Borsanyi et al. (BMW Collaboration) 2021, Nature 593:51]. The gap between the two central values is ≈144 × 10⁻¹¹, roughly an order of magnitude larger than the experimental sensitivity floor of the muon-g-2 continuation program — Fermilab E989 final at 127 ppb relative precision, equivalent to ≈1.48 × 10⁻¹⁰ absolute uncertainty [Aguillard et al. (Muon g-2 Collaboration) 2025, Phys. Rev. Lett. 135:101802], with J-PARC E34 providing an independent ultra-cold-muon cross-check. The experiment discriminates between the two calculations cleanly at design completion, and the 2025 measurement is consistent with the BMW lattice value at ≈1σ while remaining inconsistent with the 2020 dispersive value at the original ≈4σ level. The dataset distinguishes this *discriminating-experiment* shape from the *shared-parameter convergent-measurement* shape (the essay's example 1: three programs measuring the neutrino mass ordering via independent physical channels but no per-program competing point predictions). The discriminating lookup will return empty for the latter — and the empty result is informative, recording the curators' judgement that no competing point predictions of the relevant parameter satisfy the dataset's admissibility test.

> *Where to look:* the [muon-g-2 candidate-foundational program discourse card](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/discourse/muon-g-2) and the [muon-g-2 continuation program card](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/discourse/muon-g-2-continuation) — the single resolver entry carries the Theory Initiative and BMW predictions inline below the sensitivity line.

The underlying lookup is the discriminating-experiments query (tool name: `find_discriminating_experiments`), called with two experimental-program ids that share at least one target. An empty return is the curators' encoding of "no competing point predictions admissible on this pairing" and should be read as informative rather than absent.

## 5. Where does the dataset record characteristic numerical scales, and what range does each kind of physical quantity cover?

A cross-cutting question without a literature-search equivalent. The dataset has accumulated 288 numerical commitments across its frontiers, classifications, cells, predictions, and conditional consequences; each is typed by its physical dimension (energy scale, mass, length, time, coupling, dimensionless, ratio, deviation in σ) and tagged with whether the value is a lower bound, an upper bound, a two-sided central value, or a structurally-exact theoretical value. Ranked by value within a dimensional type, the dataset gives a top-down view of where a working physicist should expect new physics to live across the open landscape — gathered in one place from sources that are not normally read together.

A worked instance: rank every energy-scale entry on the dataset's open frontiers. The list spans six orders of magnitude. At the bottom, 1 TeV — twice, once as the naturalness ceiling on the supersymmetric resolution of the hierarchy problem [Martin 1997, arXiv:hep-ph/9709356], once as the naturalness ceiling on the composite-Higgs resolution [Panico–Wulzer 2016, Lect. Notes Phys. 913]. At 10⁹ GeV, the Davidson–Ibarra lower bound on the lightest right-handed Majorana neutrino mass for thermal leptogenesis to reproduce η_B ≈ 6 × 10⁻¹⁰ [Davidson–Ibarra 2002, Phys. Lett. B 535:25]. At 10¹⁰±² GeV, the Peccei–Quinn-breaking scale window for the QCD axion as a resolution of the strong-CP problem (lower edge from SN1987A axion-emission constraints, upper edge from cosmological overclosure) [Di Luzio et al. 2020, Phys. Rep. 870:1]. At 10¹¹·⁵±⁰·⁵ GeV, the narrower Peccei–Quinn window in which the QCD axion's misalignment-mechanism relic abundance reproduces the observed dark-matter density [Preskill–Wise–Wilczek 1983; Marsh 2016, Phys. Rep. 643:1]. And at 10¹⁹ GeV the Planck scale itself, the characteristic scale of the quantum-gravity frontier. A working physicist looking at this column sees, in one view, the dataset's documented "where new physics could live" landscape — and which conditional resolutions of which open questions would commit physics to which segments of it.

> *Where to look:* the [quantum-gravity frontier card](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/discourse/qg-frontier) and the [hierarchy-problem frontier card](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/discourse/hierarchy-problem) anchor the ends of the energy-scale rank; the in-between entries surface inside the "If real, implies…" sections on the dark-matter, strong-CP, matter-antimatter-asymmetry, and hierarchy-problem cards. The explorer's tile-decoration layer ("scale N") signals which classifications and frontiers carry quantitative-scale entries at a glance.

The underlying lookup is the scale-ranking query (tool name: `rank_by_scale`), called with a `kind` of physical quantity and optionally a node type to restrict to (formal classifications, open frontiers, or candidate-foundational programs) and a bound direction (lower / upper / two-sided). For exhaustive listings across every carrier surface, the related bound-search (`find_bounds`) returns the unranked list.

## 6. Which specific predictions has the dataset recorded as falsified, and what specifically excluded them?

A historical question with a structural lesson the dataset surfaces explicitly. Across the catalogue's classifications, the predictions associated with each cell carry an empirical-status tag: confirmed, unconfirmed but in tension, not yet tested, falsified, or retro-explanatory. The falsified bucket is the smallest of the five — currently seventeen entries — and reading them as a list reveals a structural fact about which classes of prediction enter it.

A worked instance: every one of the seventeen falsified predictions is a beyond-Standard-Model or near-Standard-Model overreach. Minimal SU(5) tree-level proton decay (Georgi–Glashow 1974 prediction τ_p ~ 10²⁹⁻³⁰ yr, ruled out by Super-Kamiokande's τ/B(p → e⁺ π⁰) > 2.4 × 10³⁴ yr [Takenaka et al. 2020, Phys. Rev. D 102:112011], four to five orders of magnitude beyond prediction). Heidelberg-Moscow's 0νββ claim (T₁₂ ≈ 1.5 × 10²⁵ yr in ⁷⁶Ge [Klapdor-Kleingrothaus et al. 2001], decisively excluded by GERDA's T₁₂ > 1.8 × 10²⁶ yr [Agostini et al. 2020, Phys. Rev. Lett. 125:252502]). The LSND 3+1 sterile-neutrino interpretation (excluded by IceCube atmospheric ν_μ disappearance + MicroBooNE multi-topology search). DAMA/LIBRA's WIMP interpretation of the annual modulation (excluded by XENON1T, XENONnT, LZ, and the NaI-target ANAIS-112 null result — though the modulation observable itself remains 12.9σ-established and an open puzzle, the WIMP interpretation specifically is falsified). The EDGES 21-cm cosmic-dawn absorption signal (contested by SARAS 3). The 2018 quantized Majorana-zero-mode conductance claim in InSb/Al hybrid nanowires (formally retracted; the most prominent retracted topological-matter claim). Pure MOND at galaxy-cluster scales (Bullet Cluster). TeVeS as a relativistic MOND completion (graviton-speed bound from GW170817). The DGP self-accelerating branch as a dark-energy alternative. What the dataset exposes by surfacing the bucket as a list is that settled-physics predictions definitionally do not get falsified — the entries are all beyond-Standard-Model proposals, near-BSM specific claims, or modified-gravity-class hypotheses that overcommitted on quantitative consequences and were then closed out. A working physicist scanning this list sees the structural shape of the "predictions that failed" landscape, which the citation-stating discipline preserves verbatim in each entry.

> *Where to look:* the falsified predictions surface as red-segment yield bars on the [SU(5) GUT representation content classification tile](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/fc/su5-gut-rep-content/cell-pd-pi0-eplus-falsified-GG) (proton decay) and on tile chrome across [dark-matter candidates](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/fc/dark-matter-candidates/cell-dm-DAMA-LIBRA-falsified), [neutrino-sector phenomenology](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/fc/neutrino-sector-phenomenology/cell-nu-sterile-eV-LSND-claim-FALSIFIED), [modified-gravity alternatives](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html#/fc/modified-gravity-alternatives/cell-mg-MOND-cluster-lensing), and elsewhere. Each tile's ⚠N flag counts the classification's falsified entries.

The underlying lookup is the prediction search (tool name: `find_predictions`) filtered by `status="falsified"`. Combined with `contentSearch` or `fc_id` parameters the same query narrows to a specific physics domain.

## What next

The recipes above are starting points. Each works against the [live catalogue](https://causaldynamicsemergent-gif.github.io/periodic-table-of-physics/explorer/Map_v34_explorer.html); each can be combined with the others — start with a recipe-1 experimental-coverage lookup, follow up with a recipe-3 conditional-consequences lookup on the same frontier, and the dataset's structural commitments emerge in one frame. The full set of 33 catalogue-server tools the dataset exposes covers further patterns: forced-empty cells and their closure constraints, the bears-on edges connecting structural mathematics to open frontiers, the produced-classification edges connecting experimental programs to the classifications they generated. A reader who has worked the six recipes above can read the catalogue server's tool list and recognize most of the further moves.

For physicists who would rather ask the dataset questions in natural language than invoke the catalogue queries directly, a guided chat entry point is forthcoming — see `TRACK_4_USE_SIDE_ARTIFACTS.md` §1.3. For deeper background on what the dataset is and what makes it trustworthy, the [essay](./USE_THE_MAP_ESSAY.md) is the companion document.
