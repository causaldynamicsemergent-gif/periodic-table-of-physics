## What was decided in Steps 0–2

Decisions that are NOT recoverable from the spec doc alone — captured here so a fresh chat doesn't re-litigate them.

### Spec design decisions locked in

- **`forced_by` is an array, not a single edge_id.** A cell can be forced by multiple constraints simultaneously. Recording all is more informative than picking one. `minItems: 1`, no upper bound. Order is not significant.
- **`forced_by_entry.kind` is a discriminator enum closed to `["edge"]` in Phase A.** Reserved for `"closure_constraint"` later. Authors cannot invent new `kind` values; Rule 19 will reject them. Phase A uses edges to represent closure constraints (the SM structurally-excluded workaround in §7.2 Bucket 1 of the spec).
- **`axis_mapping` is declared on the edge, not computed.** Hardlined per `PROJECT_GOAL_PREDICTIVE_LAYER.md` §3.2. Future Phase A+ chats must not propose axis-equivalence inference; the substantive correspondence claim belongs to the edge author.
- **`axis_mapping` is optional everywhere, including on bijection and categorically-equivalent edges.** The schema-level requirement was rejected because some bijection edges land in `partial` or `aspired` status where the mapping itself is what's being worked out. Rule 22 is a validator-side **warning**, not a schema-level error, so the schema bump doesn't break CI on day one before Step 5's authoring sweep runs.
- **Schema-level `additionalProperties: false` on cells preserved.** New v16 fields are declared explicitly; the strictness stays. Future Phase B/C fields on cells will need explicit declaration too — they cannot rely on `additionalProperties: true`.
- **The v15.3 → v16 transition is a one-way ratchet.** Every v15.3-conforming data file is also v16-conforming because all Phase A fields are optional. Rollback to v15.3 would be a no-op (v15.3 is a strict subset of v16-conforming data). This pattern matches the v14 → v15 transition.

- **The spec extension's Bucket 1 recommendation conflicts with Rule 20 — resolved by Step 7 via Path 1.** Surfaced during Step 3 implementation. The spec extension §7.2 (around line 290 of `MAP_v16_schema_spec_extension.md`) recommends Bucket 1 closure-constraint self-edges with `subtype: specializes` and `axis_mapping` describing the closure axis. But Rule 20 (spec §7 and Rule 20 text on line 264 of the same spec) excludes `specializes` from the forcing-subtype enum, and JSON Schema §5.3 forbids `axis_mapping` on `specializes` subtypes. Step 3 implements Rule 20 exactly as the sketch and Rule 20 text specify — `specializes` cannot force cells. **Step 7 resolution (Path 1, shipped)**: closure-constraint self-edges land with `subtype: derives-from` instead, which is in both the Rule 20 forcing set AND permits `axis_mapping`. The spec §7.2 `specializes` recommendation is superseded on this point; the binding recommendation going forward is `derives-from`. The handoff's alternative paths (lift `forced_by_entry.kind` to include `closure_constraint` as a Phase A+ schema bump per spec §9, or amend the spec) remain available for future Phase A+ work, but Path 1 turned out to require no schema changes and no spec amendment beyond the supersession note recorded in the v38 changelog. The same Path 1 resolution applies to Step 6b's Bucket 3 revisit, but the granularity choice (relabel existing `specializes` edges vs author parallel `derives-from` edges) is Step 6b's call — see the "What's queued" entry for Step 6b above.

### Worth-knowing about the v34 data

- **The data file's `_meta._schema` field already said `"Map_v16_schema.json"`** before v16 existed. Labeling drift that predated the work. As of Step 2, the label finally matches reality. A future schema bump should expect to update this field too.
- **4 pre-firewall legacy edges use `subtype: "constrains"`** — not in the v15.3 OR v16 enum (`bijection`, `derives-from`, `categorically-equivalent`, `specializes`, `composition`). `PROJECT_INFRASTRUCTURE.md` §2 explicitly tolerates them. **Do not "fix" them by adding `constrains` to the enum;** the project policy is to leave them as known legacy errors. They appear in both v15.3 and v16 validation output as 4 schema errors that are ignored by CI thresholds. The 4 edges are: `edge-xc-mtc-constrains-bootstrap`, `edge-xc-symtft-constrains-bootstrap`, `edge-xc-symtft-constrains-hcb`, `edge-xc-mtc-constrains-fqh`.
- **484 cells total** across the 30 FCs. Migration breakdown per spec §7.2:
  - Bucket 2 (`realized`): cells with `realized_examples` non-empty OR a confirmed prediction. Probably the majority — every populated SM cell, every populated FQH cell, every concrete neutron-star instance, etc.

1. `edge-xc-mckay-subgroups-lie` — `ade-su2-subgroups → ade-lie-algebras`, subtype `bijection`. McKay correspondence: irreducible representations of finite subgroups of SU(2) ↔ vertices of the affine ADE Dynkin diagram (1979).
2. `edge-xc-gabriel-quivers-lie` — `ade-quivers → ade-lie-algebras`, subtype `bijection`. 3. `edge-xc-ciz-modular-lie` — `ade-modular-invariants → ade-lie-algebras`, subtype `bijection`. Cappelli-Itzykson-Zuber (1987): modular invariant partition functions of the $\widehat{su}(2)_k$ WZW model are classified by ADE simply-laced Lie algebras with Coxeter number $k+2$.

The headline numbers above (mathematician, year, theorem identification) are orienting cues, not authoring text. The Step 5 chat is responsible for verifying each claim against primary or canonical secondary references before naming it in a `correspondence` field, and for citing in `description` text only what those references actually say.

**Authoring process per edge.** For each of the five edges:

1. **Read the source and target FCs.** Use `Map of Physics:get_node` on the `from` and `to` IDs (the MCP server is still on v15.3 / v34, but `classification_axes` and `cells` are v15.3-vintage fields, so they work). 3. **Compose each entry.** Shape per spec §4:

   {
     "from_axis": "<axis name in source FC's classification_axes>",
     "to_axis":   "<axis name in target FC's classification_axes>",
     "correspondence": "<short name of the math theorem, e.g. 'McKay correspondence', 'Gabriel theorem', 'identity'>",
     "description": "<one-line gloss, optional but recommended>"

   `correspondence` is free text; the spec calls it "the edge author's substantive claim, not validator-checked." Keep it short and citation-able. `"identity"` is a valid value when the two axes are literally the same enumeration.
4. **Add or extend the `citations` array on the edge** with the canonical reference (McKay 1980, Gabriel 1972, Cappelli-Itzykson-Zuber 1987, Bridgeland-King-Reid 2001, etc.). The self-check is on the record, not just informal; the PR description should state that it was applied.

## Lessons from Steps 0–7

- **The raw CDN negative-response cache is real.** During Step 2, `web_fetch` returned a 404 on `Map_v15_3_schema.json` even though the file was sitting in the repo. The github.com blob HTML fallback worked (handoff documents this in `EXPLORER_HANDOFF.md`). Current payload path in May 2026: `data["payload"]["codeViewBlobLayoutRoute.StyledBlob"]["rawLines"]`. Probe the structure with a small inspector before assuming the path — GitHub web-app refactors keep the data accessible but rename the route.

- **The v34 dataset's `_meta._schema` field already said `"Map_v16_schema.json"`** before v16 existed. Caught during Step 2 by inspecting the file during real-data validation. Was a labeling drift; resolved when v16 actually shipped. **Lesson for future schema bumps:** the `_meta._schema` field is the authoritative pointer in the data file, and it can drift ahead of the actual schema. Check it explicitly at the start of any schema-bump work.

- **The Step 1 validator was pure JSON Schema; Step 3 added the first validator-side rules.** Inspecting the v15.3 `validate.py` revealed that despite the v15.3 schema declaring `_validator_side_rules` 4–14, the Python script did NOT implement them as separate checks — it only ran `Draft202012Validator` and partitioned the resulting errors into legacy vs new. Rules 4–14 are JSON-Schema-expressible (and are expressed in the schema's `allOf` blocks), so the partition-only approach validated them correctly via the schema itself. **Rule 19 onward changes that pattern:** rules 19, 20, 21, 23 cross-reference between cells and edges, which JSON Schema cannot do natively (no `$ref` from one part of the document to another), and Rule 22 is warning-level which JSON Schema has no concept of. Step 3 therefore added the first Python-side rule functions to the codebase. Future schema bumps that introduce more cross-reference rules should follow the same `run_validator_side_rules → (errors, warnings)` pattern; the `_build_indices` helper provides the lookup dicts.

- **Warning-level rules need their own output channel and pass-through pass/fail logic.** Rule 22's warning level is intentionally distinct from CI-failing errors. The validator now reports three counts (legacy errors, new schema errors + rule errors, warnings) and the PASS line includes the warning count tail (e.g., "PASS — dataset validates cleanly (legacy errors within tolerance) (5 warnings)."). Phase B and Phase C will likely introduce more warning-level rules (e.g., `if_real_implies` consistency checks); the same channel handles them without further changes to the output skeleton.

- **The spec's "OR a confirmed prediction" promotion clause is empty on v34.** Surfaced during Step 4 authoring. The spec extension §7.2 Bucket 2 rule says a cell gets `constructive_status: realized` if `realized_examples` is non-empty OR a confirmed prediction exists. Inspection of v34 found 12 inline cell-scoped `confirmed` predictions across 12 distinct cells — all 12 already had non-empty `realized_examples`. FC-level `predictive_yield` predictions (111 confirmed entries) are not associated with specific cells, so the strict spec reading does not let them promote individual cells. Net effect: the OR clause adds zero cells beyond the `realized_examples` path. Step 4 landed the strict reading explicitly to keep the rule unambiguous; if a future physicist reviewer wants to promote a specific cell on the strength of a confirmed prediction alone, that's a one-line revision PR. **Lesson for future spec authoring:** when a Bucket / classification rule has an OR clause, verify against real data whether the OR clause is actually load-bearing before landing the spec; if it isn't on day one, document the population it's reserved for so reviewers know what to look for.

- **`json.dumps(data, indent=2, ensure_ascii=False)` is byte-identical to the v34 file's existing formatting.** Verified during Step 4 by round-tripping the unmodified data through `json.load → json.dump`. This means Python's standard formatter is sufficient for clean diffs on the data file — no custom formatter needed (unlike the v16 schema, which required `/home/claude/v16-build/compact_format.py` to avoid line-count drift). The data file uses no compact-inline-objects style, so the standard formatter handles it. **Lesson for future data-file edits:** start with a round-trip test to confirm the standard formatter matches the on-disk style; if it does, edits-then-dump produces minimal diffs automatically. The `ensure_ascii=False` flag is required to preserve characters like `ℤ`, `§`, and `→` that appear in cell content.

- **JSON Schema 2020-12 conditional `allOf` + `if`/`then` rules compose well.** All four Phase A conditional rules use this pattern; the 29-assertion test suite confirms it works as expected. The pattern is: `{ "if": { properties + required }, "then": { required or properties-with-false } }`. The `"properties": { "fieldname": false }` idiom is the JSON Schema 2020-12 way to forbid a field conditionally.

- **Compact JSON formatting matters for git diffs.** Initial output of `json.dump(indent=2)` doubled the file length vs the v15.3 hand-formatted style (1826 lines vs 786). A custom formatter that keeps small leaf objects on one line (≤ 3 keys, all values primitive, fits under 80 chars) brought v16 down to 934 lines — a clean ~148-line delta showing the actual additions. **The custom-formatter pattern is in `/home/claude/v16-build/compact_format.py`** in the Step 2 workspace; reusable for future schema bumps. Apply it as a final post-process step after `json.dump(indent=2)` produces the canonical-but-verbose output.

- **`additionalProperties: false` on cells is load-bearing.** Without explicit declaration of `constructive_status` and `forced_by` in the cell `$def`, the new fields would be rejected even though they're new optional fields. The strictness is correct (catches typos and prevents schema drift) but it means every Phase B/C cell field must also be declared explicitly — they cannot rely on permissive defaults.

- **The closure-constraint problem is real and Phase A handles it by workaround.** Phase A's `forced_by_entry.kind` enum is closed to `["edge"]`. Bucket 1 (the SM structurally-excluded cells) needs to point at closure constraints that are not currently first-class nodes. The spec §7.2 workaround: encode each closure constraint as a self-edge on the SM-rep-content FC with `subtype: specializes` and `from = to = sm-rep-content`. This satisfies the validator (the edge exists and has an applicable subtype) without inventing a new node type. A future Phase A+ schema bump could introduce `kind: "closure_constraint"` and a `closure-constraint` node type, but that decision is deferred until the workaround actually causes friction.

- **Schema validation requires careful handling of `$ref` resolution.** The Step 2 unit tests used `jsonschema.Draft202012Validator` with a `Registry` and `Resource` from the `referencing` library to resolve `#/$defs/cell` and `#/$defs/edge` references. Pattern: load the schema, register it under a URN, build the validator with `{"$ref": "urn:schema#/$defs/<name>"}`. The naïve `Draft202012Validator(schema["$defs"]["cell"])` approach loses the `$defs` context and fails on any internal `$ref` like `$ref: #/$defs/prediction`. The working pattern is in `/home/claude/v16-build/test_v16_schema.py` and `validate_v34_against_v16.py`; reusable for future schema work.

- **One-way ratchet design is robust.** The v15.3 → v16 transition is strictly additive — every v15.3-conforming file validates against v16 with zero new errors. The data file doesn't need migration to validate; only to *use* the new fields does authoring happen. This decouples schema-bump readiness from authoring readiness. The same pattern should be used for Phase B and Phase C schema bumps.

- **`correspondence` records the shape of the mapping, not the named theorem.** Surfaced during Step 5 authoring. The spec extension §4 lists `"identity"` and `"McKay correspondence"` side-by-side as example values, and on a literal reading either could be used for a McKay-correspondence edge linking two cartan-type axes ranging over the same A/D/E enumeration. The handoff's tie-breaker (this file's §"Subtleties the Step 5 chat should know about") is binding: when the two axes are literally the same enumeration paired pointwise, the `correspondence` value is `"identity"`, and the named theorem (McKay, Gabriel, CIZ, BKR/KV, McKay-quiver) lives in the entry's `description` field and the edge's `citations` array. The reason is downstream-tool readability: `find_forced_cells` (planned for Step 8) needs to know mechanically that an A-series cell on the source forces an A-series cell on the target — that's identity behavior. Reading "McKay correspondence" as a free-text string, the tool cannot infer pointwise pairing without a lookup table of theorem names. Reading "identity," it applies pairing directly. **Lesson for future axis-mapping authoring**: `correspondence` is the *kind* of transformation (identity / dual / duality-twist / Verlinde-conjugation / ...), not the *name* of the theorem. Two edges authored by different sweeps should share `correspondence` values whenever they share mapping behavior, regardless of which theorem each cites.

- **Authoring scope is set by what the named theorem actually pairs, not by how many axes exist.** Surfaced on the CIZ edge during Step 5. The source FC (`ade-modular-invariants`) has two axes (`cartan-type` and `level`), the target (`ade-lie-algebras`) has only `cartan-type`. The Cappelli-Itzykson-Zuber classification pairs ADE Cartan types directly and constrains the level (k+2 = Coxeter number of the matching Lie algebra) — but the level-side of that constraint has no counterpart axis on `ade-lie-algebras` to map to. Authoring choice that landed: map only `cartan-type → cartan-type`, with one entry, and record the level-side constraint as prose in the entry's `description`. The schema permits one entry (per `minItems: 1`); the spec's "not every axis must be mapped" guidance applies. **Lesson**: an `axis_mapping` array's length should match the number of axes the named theorem actually establishes a pairing for, not the number of axes either FC declares. Constraints that aren't axis-to-axis pairings (like "the Lie algebra's Coxeter number determines the WZW level") go in `description` text and edge prose, not in fabricated mapping entries.

- **Existing edge content is load-bearing and edits are append-only.** Per the project's working norm, Step 5 did not touch any existing `description`, `subtype`, `type`, `from`, `to`, or pre-existing citation on the 5 target edges. The `axis_mapping` array was appended at the end of each edge's property block (matching the v16 schema's property declaration order, which places `axis_mapping` last), and one citation was appended to the existing `citations` array on the BKR/Du Val edge. **Lesson**: per-edge authoring sweeps should default to append-only on the existing structure unless the existing structure is itself a recorded loose end. The 5 edges' existing descriptions already correctly identify each theorem; the `axis_mapping` arrays add the machine-readable correspondence claim without overwriting the prose statement of it.

- **Kapranov-Vasserot 2000 was the appropriate canonical addition on the BKR edge.** Surfaced during Step 5 authoring. The BKR/Du Val edge previously cited only Bridgeland-King-Reid 2001. The Step 5 handoff named the correspondence as "Bridgeland-King-Reid / Kapranov-Vasserot equivalence" because Kapranov-Vasserot 2000 (Math. Ann. 316, 565) is the ADE-Du-Val-specific predecessor: BKR 2001 generalized the equivalence to higher-dimensional crepant resolutions, while KV 2000 established it specifically for Kleinian / Du Val singularities. Since the authored `correspondence` description on the edge now names both, both citations are appropriate. **Lesson**: when an axis-mapping `description` names a paired-author theorem (X / Y equivalence), the edge's `citations` should cover both authors' canonical references; a one-citation edge whose `axis_mapping description` names two theorems is internally inconsistent.

- **Diff shape predicted by the handoff matched almost exactly.** The Step 5 sketch predicted "30–80 added lines total." The actual diff against the v35 baseline was 47 added / 5 removed, dead-center of the predicted range. **Lesson**: when a handoff has a diff-shape prediction, it functions as a sanity check on the authoring sweep — a Step that overshoots the prediction by 5× should pause and audit before committing, because something out-of-scope likely got swept in. Step 5 stayed in scope and the diff confirms it.

- **The v36 file remains under the same `Map_v34_consolidated.json` filename.** Per `PROJECT_INFRASTRUCTURE.md` §2 the filename is stable from v34 onward; the version bump is internal (`_meta.version`) and via git tag, not by filename change. Future schema and data bumps continue to follow this convention. **Lesson restated for the record**: the era of `MAP_vN_*.json` filenames is closed; the consolidated file's filename does not change.

- **The spec's Bucket-naming Rule-20 conflict is on both Bucket 1 AND Bucket 3, not just Bucket 1.** Surfaced during Step 6. The Step 2 / Step 3 documentation flagged the Bucket 1 conflict in detail (spec §7.2 names `subtype: specializes` for closure-constraint self-edges, but Rule 20 forbids `specializes` from forcing). The Bucket 3 case is structurally identical: spec §7.2 names "the SM↔SU(5) cross-classification edge" as the forcing edge, but the actual edge in the dataset is `edge-xc-sm-specializes-su5-gut` with `subtype: specializes` — same Rule 20 fail. The handoff and the spec both missed this in advance. **Lesson**: when the spec lists Bucket migrations that depend on existing dataset edges, do a pre-flight check on every named edge's `subtype` against Rule 20 before drafting Step entries that assume the edges are usable. The two-FC-bucket pattern (Bucket 1, Bucket 3) suggests that whatever resolution Step 7 lands for Rule 20 / `specializes` will need to apply to BOTH the Bucket 1 closure-constraint authoring AND a future Bucket 3b revisit; the resolutions cannot be made independently.

- **"Empty binding set" is a legitimate Step outcome, not a Step failure.** Surfaced during Step 6. The Step 5 sketch said "the binding set comes from inspecting the actual cells" — Step 6 took that literally and shipped the inspection finding (binding set = ∅) as the deliverable, with no cell annotations changed. The firewall §2.5 self-check is what makes this the right call: "Would I author this annotation on physics-content grounds alone?" If the only available forcing edge is Rule-20-excluded and the candidate cells are already classified differently, the answer is no — and the firewall-honest move is to record the finding, not to find SOMETHING to author. **Lesson**: future Bucket-style steps should accept "empty binding set" as a possible outcome rather than expanding scope to produce annotations. A no-op data file bump that records WHY no annotations were authored is more useful than an off-spec annotation that has to be unwound later.

- **Step 4's strict reading is load-bearing in two directions, not one.** Step 4 documented the strict `realized_examples`-non-empty → `realized` rule with the caveat "if a physicist reviewer thinks a specific cell deserves `realized` on the strength of a confirmed prediction alone, that's a one-line revision on a future PR — the field is reversible at trivial cost." The implicit assumption was that the only revisit pressure would come from "this cell should be promoted to realized." Step 6 surfaced the symmetric pressure: the strict reading also classified `cell-su5-24-NA` (whose `realized_examples` mixes observed SM bosons and unobserved X/Y leptoquarks in a single entry) as `realized`, consuming the only SU(5)-FC candidate that would otherwise have qualified for Bucket 3. The revision direction is "this cell should be demoted from realized to conjectured-by-pattern" — same one-line revision, opposite direction. **Lesson**: strict-reading decisions in early Bucket steps can foreclose later Bucket assignments. A future Bucket migration should explicitly consider the interplay between bucket assignments before treating each bucket as independent. The `cell-su5-24-NA` reclassification is exactly the kind of "one-line revision" Step 4 anticipated; Step 6 cannot make that decision unilaterally because it requires user direction on what `realized_examples` means when the example mixes statuses.

- **Handoff "candidate" lists need to be cross-referenced against the actual cell-id structure, not treated as binding scope.** The Step 6 candidate list — "X/Y bosons, monopoles, predicted proton-decay channels" — was natural-language shorthand from the handoff author for what should land in Bucket 3. In the actual cell layout: X/Y bosons share `cell-su5-24-NA` with SM gauge bosons (no dedicated cell); monopoles live in `cell-em-monopole-EM-empty` of `electromagnetic-phenomena-across-energy-regimes` (not in `su5-gut-rep-content`); predicted proton-decay channels live in `proton-decay-searches` (their own FC). The handoff's qualifier "the binding set comes from inspecting the actual cells" was load-bearing — without that qualifier the Step 6 chat would have been pushed toward authoring annotations that don't fit any actual cell. **Lesson**: candidate lists in handoff "What's queued" entries should be treated as orientation hints, not as the binding scope of the step. The binding scope is always the cells in the named FC, identified by `cell_id`, classified per the spec's literal rules.

- **The diff-shape prediction pattern works for no-op steps too.** Step 5's lesson noted that handoff diff-shape predictions function as sanity checks; an overshoot signals scope creep. Step 6 demonstrates the converse: a no-op step has a tiny predictable diff (the `_state` line + `version`/`previous_version` pair + one appended changelog entry + the trailing-comma flip on the previously-last changelog entry). A no-op step whose diff includes any actual content edit should pause and audit before committing — that's the signal that an inspection finding was silently expanded into content authoring. Step 6 stayed within the no-op envelope.

- **The Path 1 resolution worked exactly as predicted; the substantive Step 7 design call was the number of closure-constraint edges, not the subtype.** Surfaced during Step 7. The handoff predicted Path 1 (`derives-from` self-edges) would resolve the Rule 20 conflict, and it did — Rule 20 accepted the edges, Rule 21 was satisfied automatically by the self-edge construction (`from = to = host FC`), Rule 22 stayed silent because `derives-from` is outside its trigger set, Rule 23 accepted the `axis_mapping` once `generation` was used as both `from_axis` and `to_axis`. The schema-level §5.3 rule that forbids `axis_mapping` on `specializes`/`composition` was confirmed inactive on `derives-from`. The actual authoring decision — once Path 1 was locked in — was how many closure-constraint edges to author: one (one structural claim total), two (fermion-N/A vs non-fermion-generation), three (fermion-N/A vs gauge-no-generation vs Higgs-no-generation), or seventeen (one per cell). Step 7 landed three on physics-content grounds: the 17 cells' own `description` fields cluster into exactly three structural claims (fermion replicates over generations; gauge boson multiplicity fixed by dim(G); minimal Higgs sector is one doublet), each with its own canonical-textbook citation set, and merging them would hide distinct physics mechanisms behind a shared closure outcome. **Lesson**: when a Rule resolution is locked in by the handoff (Path 1), the live Step's substantive design decision shifts to the next layer (closure-constraint granularity). The granularity choice should be made on physics-content grounds — what does the cell-level `description` evidence already cluster into? — not on schema-minimization grounds (which would push toward one merged edge to minimize line count).

- **Self-edges on a formal-classification node are first-class in v16; no schema work was required to introduce them.** Surfaced during Step 7. Before Step 7, the dataset had 0 self-edges out of 186 total (97 cross-classification + 89 other). The v16 schema places no restriction on `from == to`; the only restrictions are Rule 21 (`from` or `to` must equal the host FC for forcing edges, which a self-edge satisfies trivially with `from = to = host`) and the §5.3 / §5.4 / Rule 22 / Rule 23 rules already in place. The self-edge introduces a new structural idiom in the dataset (a FC pointing to itself to record a closure constraint on its own axes), but it required no schema bump and no validator change. The MCP server's `find_cross_classification` will return these self-edges alongside the cross-FC edges; downstream UI code that filters cross-classification edges should be aware that some now have `from == to`. **Lesson**: the closure-constraint pattern documented in spec §7.2 is implementable today as `derives-from` self-edges, without invoking the Phase A+ `closure_constraint` kind. The Phase A+ schema work remains a candidate for the future, but only if the self-edge idiom causes friction in practice (e.g., if downstream tooling specifically conflates self-edges with non-self-edges in a way that loses information). At present the self-edge idiom is the simpler path and Step 7 used it.

- **The spec's `axis_mapping` recommendation on closure-constraint edges is load-bearing for machine queryability, not just human-readable description.** Surfaced during Step 7. The spec §7.2 calls for `axis_mapping describing the closure axis` on the closure-constraint self-edges. A reading that minimized the axis_mapping ("self-edges don't really have axis correspondences — from and to are the same FC with the same axes, so axis_mapping doesn't add information beyond what `description` conveys") would have omitted it. Step 7 included it because the AI-first goal (`PROJECT_GOAL_SUPPLEMENT.md` §1.2) wants the closure axis to be machine-queryable: a future `find_forced_cells({by_axis: "generation"})` query needs the closure axis declared as data, not as natural-language prose. The `correspondence: "closure-constraint"` value Step 7 introduced signals to downstream tooling that this is the closure pattern, not a McKay-style bijection; the value can be added to a downstream `correspondence` enum if a future Phase A+ schema bump wants to lock down the set. **Lesson**: `axis_mapping` is the machine-readable channel for the substantive correspondence claim; if a closure constraint declares its closure axis in prose only, the closure becomes invisible to MCP tooling. The Step 5 `correspondence: "identity"` lesson and the Step 7 `correspondence: "closure-constraint"` introduction together establish that `correspondence` is an open-vocabulary field where downstream tooling reads specific values; future authoring should reuse `"closure-constraint"` for parallel patterns rather than coining new strings.

- **Three citable structural claims, two shared citations.** Surfaced during Step 7. All three closure-constraint edges cite the same two sources (PDG + Weinberg QFT II §21.3) because those two references jointly cover all three structural claims (fermion-generation replication, gauge-boson multiplicity from gauge-group dimension, single-Higgs-doublet convention) — each a textbook SM fact. Step 5's lesson noted that "an edge's `citations` should cover the named theorem"; Step 7 extends that: when multiple edges encode different structural claims that share a citation set (because they all derive from one canonical reference), the citations may be duplicated across edges. The duplication is not redundancy — it's the substantive claim that each edge's structural content can be verified against the named sources. **Lesson**: edge citations are content-claims, not author-attribution; two edges citing the same source are claiming that source supports their respective structural assertions. A future audit that finds duplicated citations across edges should check whether the citations support each edge's specific content, not flag the duplication as a hygiene issue.

- **Step 7's resolution path becomes the precedent for Step 6b but the granularity choice is fresh.** Surfaced during Step 7's planning. The Rule 20 resolution (Path 1: `derives-from`) carries forward to Step 6b — both forcing edges Step 6b touches (`edge-xc-sm-specializes-su5-gut` and `edge-xc-pd-specializes-su5`) need either subtype-relabeling or parallel `derives-from` edges to become usable for forcing. But Step 7's *granularity* choice (three edges grouped by physics mechanism) does not transfer mechanically: the SU(5)-GUT and proton-decay edges are existing cross-FC edges with their own physics-content claims, not new self-edges Step 7 authored. Step 6b's design call is whether to relabel the existing edges (which rewrites their semantics) or author parallel edges (which adds redundancy). Step 7 did not pre-commit to either path; it intentionally left the choice to the Step 6b chat because the right answer depends on what `specializes` was intended to record on those edges (a claim Step 7 did not investigate). **Lesson**: when a Step's resolution sets a precedent for a future Step, the precedent is the *resolution mechanism*, not the full authoring approach. Step 6b inherits Path 1 as the resolution mechanism; it must make its own physics-content decisions about edge granularity and existing-edge handling.
